## Within a team

### Bytesize Architecture Sessions
#### Overview

Bytesize Architecture Sessions are a workshop format that helps teams understand the systems they work on. Each session focuses on a small slice of a system. After some sessions your team will become consistent in the understanding of their systems, grow a common vocabulary and ultimately build tools to design the future together.

Bytesize Architecture sessions are used in decision-making to facilitate collaborative software design through a structured workshop format that prioritises shared understanding. This approach involves small groups of eight to ten people engaging in repeated one-hour modelling sessions

Using these sessions is like assembling a map of the territory where every team member holds a different piece of the landscape. By sketching their sections individually first, less detail is lost before converging them into a single, improved guide for the journey ahead.

### Decision Records

The idea behind ADRs is to record _significant_ architectural decisions that is both short and it follows an accepted template. Recording decisions helps with making better decisions, also we end up with a quick reference to understand what has been done in the past.

## Personal

### Left Hand Column

The left-hand column is a reflective tool developed by Dr Chris Argyris designed to help individuals understand the hidden assumptions and internal dialogues that occur during communication. It is particularly useful for handling difficult conversations, managing awkward meetings, and processing power-related stress.

To use this technique, you typically document a conversation by splitting a page into columns:
* The Right-Hand Column: What was said, facts
* The Left-Hand Column: Your record what you were really thinking and feeling but did not express at the time

The ultimate objective is to become conscious of the dichotomy between words and thoughts

![Left Side Column ]({{site.images}}/2026/left-side.png)


#### Why use this tool, in the context of better decision making

* Understand power dynamics, starting with self awareness. This tool allows you to pause and inspect your reactions and those of others.
*  Improving Knowledge Flow: Decisions require asking the right questions and understanding the problem from multiple perspectives. By inspecting your internal dialogue, you can identify where communication is breaking down, which is essential for knowledge transfer and innovation within a group of programmers

### Influence Mapping

#### Overview

Influence Mapping is a visual tool used to navigate the "decision environment" surrounding a central decision-maker, or "the D". It addresses the ["Sir Galahad Fallacy"](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/charleslambdin_good-work-does-not-go-unnoticed-if-thats-activity-7311063535705300993-zIbO), the mistaken belief that technical facts and "better arguments" alone are sufficient to persuade others. Since architectural choices are often a record of hidden power structures, this tool helps externalise complex socio-technical forces.

Some key steps:
* Listing and Casting: Identifying the authority figure ("D") and categorising stakeholders as Coalition, Opposition, or co-players.
* Rating Metrics: Plotting these individuals on a matrix based on their level of support and their organisational influence.
* Visualising Relationships: Drawing arrows to show influence paths, where line thickness represents "permission", the level of receptivity one person has toward another‚Äôs influence.

Ultimately, influence mapping treats decision-making as a "team sport". It allows architects to evolve the broader decision landscape and leverage existing dynamics, ensuring that technical proposals are taken seriously within the organisation‚Äôs "social milieu"
